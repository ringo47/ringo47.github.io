Compact Passive Smartphone Stabilizer

A phone case designed to isolate high-frequency vibrations from the camera while the user is walking and running - resulting in a less blurry picture.

The motivation for our product is to bridge the gap between the growing amount of smartphone cameras and the lack of photo equipment available for those cameras. Smartphone cameras are the most popular form of photography in the world. Yet, as users of smartphone cameras, we were tired of the inefficiency of the process. Everytime we try to take photos, a large amount of photos end up getting deleted because of blurriness from unexpected motion. In conducting customer surveys, we found that most smartphone camera users shared our struggle. Furthermore, our market research of current competitors showed that the market leaders were not satisfying the customer needs due to a combination of price and product user interface. The conventional solution to the instability of smartphone cameras in competitor products has been to use a three axis gimbal system, resulting in a long rod design that is inconvenient to carry, intrusive to adjacent people, and a struggle to use. According to the results of our customer survey, these are also biggest reasons why most people aren’t currently using photo equipment. Our motivation for this project is to solve this inefficiency of smartphone cameras, and bridge the gap between consumer needs and market solutions.

SCOT-T Planetary Exploration CubeRover

SCOT-T is the first of the CubeRover© class used for space exploration. Designed to be compact and lightweight, this small rover is the first iteration for Astrobotic's mission to complete the Lunar X-Prize.

CubeRovers are envisioned as a class of small, lightweight, economical exploration robots. They are surface-roving counterparts to CubeSats, as transformational paradigms for more frequent missions, tolerating higher explorational risk and cutting cost. CubeRovers will proliferate as diverse means to explore more with less. In this project, I was the lead structures engineer, conducting hand analysis and FEA on the majority of the parts for mass reduction. I also machined the majority of the parts with a CNC Mill.

Stiffness Tuning with SMA

Stiffness Tuning with a Partially-Embedded Shape-Memory-Alloy Coil

Current elastomers are limited in the sense that they will typically have a set effective stiffness. By being able to control the effective stiffness of an elastomer, it is possible for soft mechanisms and robots to have much more controllable degrees of freedom. To do this, shape-memory-alloy coil, or SMA coil, is partially embedded into a silicone elastomer. The stiffness of the elastomer is controlled by the temperature of the SMA coil, where after the SMA coil's activation temperature, it will begin to exert a restorning force, hence increasing the effective stiffness. To increase the response time of the elastomer, the SMA coil is partially embedded into the silicone, where it can cool down much more quickly if exposed to ambient air temperature. A theoretical model is validated with experimental data, where the effective stiffness is compared against the temperature of the SMA coil.

PCKMAN

Lower Limb Prosthetic Socket Interface using Jamming of Granular Material

We evaluate a novel type of lower-limb check socket that utilizes granular jamming of material that can accommodate large volume and shape variations of the residual limb to increase conformability and comfort for recent amputees. Investigations across shells of different stiffness, grains of different size, and packing conditions under different vacuum pressures have shown that a stiff shell made of 1.5 mm thick carbon fiber, coffee grains with a minimum vacuum pressure of 15 inHg can withstand 1053 kPa of statically applied pressure with less than 1 mm of compression. The overall socket could also be as light as 3 lbs once an onboard vacuum is attached and accommodate a roughly 7\% change in limb volume, showing that further research in a check socket that utilizes granular jamming of material could allow more conformability and comfort throughout the day for recent amputees.

Design of a Configurable Anthropomorphic Underactuated Hand

This research project aims to reduce the number of actuators in a prosthetic hand while retaining the number of controllable degrees-of-freedom.

We present the design and fabrication of a configurable anthropomorphic underactuated hand for multipurposemanipulation tasks. In order to increase the versatility ofan underactuated hand, a mechanical selector wasintroduced to individually control the actuation for each of the four tendon-driven fingers. The selector is controlledby a servo motor and the grasping motion is controlled by a DC motor. The body of the hand and fingers are 3D-printed from a rigid plastic while the antagonistic passiveelastic members for the fingers are a silicone elastomer.Lastly, the hand uses a differential pulley system for an adaptive grasp.

Fiber Optically Sensorized Multi-Fingered Gripper

This project was to demonstrate how embedded fiber optic sensors in the structure of 3D-printed fingers could revolutionize sensing in future prothestic hands.

We present the design, fabrication, and characterization of a fiber optically sensorized robotic hand for multi purpose manipulation tasks. The robotic hand has three fingers that enable both pinch and power grips. The main bone structure was made of a rigid plastic material and covered by soft skin. Both bone and skin contain embedded fiber optics for force and tactile sensing, respectively. Eight fiber optic strain sensors were used for rigid bone force sensing, and six fiber optic strain sensors were used for soft skin tactile sensing. For characterization, different loads were applied in two orthogonal axes at the fingertip and the sensor signals were measured from the bone structure. The skin was also characterized by applying a light load on different places for contact localization. The actuation of the hand was achieved by a tendon-driven under-actuated system. Gripping motions are implemented using an active tendon located on the volar side of each finger and connected to a motor. Opening motions of the hand were enabled by passive elastic tendons located on the dorsal side of each finger.
